Papers

MLSB 2025

Self Distillation Fine-Tuning of Protein Language Models Improves Versatility in Protein Design

Amin Tavakoli*, Raswanth Murugan*, Ozan Gokdemir, Arvind Ramanathan, Frances H Arnold, Anima Anandkumar.

We propose a general and efficient supervised fine-tuning (SFT) framework for protein language models (PLMs) to improve the fidelity and functional relevance of generated sequences while controlling their diversity. Due to the limited availability of experimentally annotated data, our method constructs training sets directly from the PLM using lightweight, domain-specific biophysical filters. These filters both curate high-quality sequences and identify candidates for experimental validation. Applied to a genome-scale PLM (GenSLM) on the tryptophan synthase family, this approach yields more novel sequences with improved stability and design-relevant properties.

ICML 2024

MolGene-E: Inverse Molecular Design to Modulate Single Cell Transcriptomics

Rahul Ohlan*, Raswanth Murugan*, Li Xie, Mohammadsadeq Mottaqi, Shuo Zhang, and Lei Xie.

We propose MolGene-E (like Dall-E but for drugs discovery), a deep learning framework that designs new drugs capable of restoring diseased cells to a healthy state. While single-cell data reveals the precise differences between sick and healthy cells, its extreme noise and complexity currently prevent machine learning methods from using it for drug design. MolGene-E overcomes this by first cleaning and aligning the noisy cellular data, then using a generative model to create novel molecules based on that clear genetic profile. Validated by CRISPR experiments, our method consistently outperforms existing baselines, establishing a state-of-the-art tool for modern drug discovery.

AAMAS 2023

Intelligent Onboard Routing in Stochastic Dynamic Environments using Transformers

Rohit Chowdhury, Raswanth Murugan, and Deepak Subramani.

We propose a Decision Transformer-based framework for the optimal onboard routing of autonomous marine agents. Traditional Reinforcement Learning (RL) is often too computationally expensive for real-time re-planning in complex ocean environments. Our method solves this by reframing RL path planning as a supervised sequence modeling task. We train the transformer offline using state-action-return datasets generated by solving stochastic dynamically orthogonal Hamilton-Jacobi equations. In real-time, the model processes the agent's spatio-temporal state sequences to infer ocean flows and efficiently map optimal routes. Evaluations show our approach enables robust, real-time navigation that resists noise and generalizes to entirely novel flow scenarios.

My Lore

The Question

F or as long as I can remember—even as far back as the second grade—my grandfather would ask me some variation of the same question: "What is the plan? What do you want to do in life?" I started with the usual answers: astronaut, engineer, fireman, CEO. But as I grew older, he expected more of a plan. A single word no longer sufficed. To avoid the inevitable follow-up questions, I figured out that if I gave him highly niche, specific things that mattered to me that particular week, he wouldn't know how to probe further. So, my "purpose" became catching Arceus in Pokémon Platinum, mastering the Cat's in the Cradle yo-yo trick, or figuring out a backflip. He wasn't essentially thrilled about those answers, but he'd let me off the hook. The only thing that was absolutely unacceptable to him was silence. I had to give an answer of some sort.

But when he passed away, something shifted. Perhaps in retrospect, there exists a kind of magic to it, but almost overnight, I inherited his voice. That question became my own. It grew louder, especially because, for the first time, I was stepping out from under the shelter of established institutions into the outside world. It was the only thing I had to guide my next decisions, so I held onto it deeply.

If I’m honest, the rest of this lore is just a TL;DR of me trying to find a better answer. I haven't found the one yet, but I've realized a little more about myself. I think I'm a non-conformist. I'm curious. I am tenacious. By nature I am a Bene Gesserit but by nurture learnt the ways of a Mentat (For the non-Dune readers: people skills and intuition came naturally to me, but I had to actively discover my love for thinking in structures, and logic). I navigate life by process of elimination, driven far more by what I reject than what I seek, making the few times that I do seek very compelling.

In between the CV lines

Growing up in Bangalore, India’s Silicon Valley, I was surrounded by software engineers. Honestly, it seemed boring—an endless loop of creating problems within the four perimeters of a screen just to solve them. What completely flipped my perspective was an opportunity in my freshman year at IIT to build an autonomous pesticide-spraying robot for Indian farmers. We were doing engineering exactly how it looks in the movies: building the chassis, soldering connections, slotting in the battery, 3D printing the wheels, and coding the entire thing end-to-end. In hindsight, I think everyone should do a project like that to figure out what flavor of engineering they actually like. For me, the most fascinating realization was that a few lines of code inside the microcontroller served as the brain, dictating exactly how the machine moved and interacted with the dirt. That hooked me. I decided I wanted to build more systems like that, so I switched my major to Computer Science. (Hearing that the CS majors were being taught about Hilbert’s Hotel was also a solid bonus point for the switch).

Before I could dive deeper into physical robotics, COVID happened and the world shut down. Fortunately, I managed to land a remote internship at the Indian Institute of Science (IISc). Working at the intersection of ocean physics and reinforcement learning, we were trying to develop autonomous onboard ocean routing algorithms. That stint expanded my horizons. I realized AI didn’t need metal and motors to interact with the real world; there was this broader frontier called AI for Science where you could tackle massive, tangible physical systems.

There was some overlap in my career timelines, and during this period, I also started working at Samsung R&D. Between the lab and the industry, I saw firsthand the unreasonable effectiveness of AI. Whether it was scientific routing or consumer products, AI possessed this unique ability to deliver first- and second-degree tangible impacts on the real world and the people using it.

But my biggest breakthrough didn't happen in a lab or an office; it happened in Bangalore traffic. To survive the grueling commute and numb my mind, I started listening to audiobooks. I picked up The Emperor of All Maladies by Dr. Siddhartha Mukherjee (I highly recommend it—read all of his books, he has an absolute flair for the written word). That book officially broke a mental block for me. Biology had felt so distant—the last time I truly studied it was in the 10th grade—that I couldn't fathom making an impact there. But Mukherjee placed high hopes on algorithms, suggesting they might not eradicate cancer, but could make it as tractable as diabetes is today.

That inherited question flared up again. I knew I needed to figure out what this field was about and if I could contribute. The problem was my absolute zero background in the subject. I sent out 50 cold emails until a professor at the City University of New York responded. For four months, I worked my day job at Samsung during the week, and spent my weekends and nights researching diffusion models for docking. Eventually, I was offered a fully paid visiting scientist position in his lab. I packed my bags and moved to NYC.

That year was profoundly formative, both personally and professionally. I learned a lot about what worked, but more importantly, I learned what didn't work. I realized that scientific data is inherently sparse, noisy, and expensive to generate. You cannot just apply standard deep learning and expect a miracle. You have to build models from the ground up, baking in inductive biases and intrinsic knowledge about the physical system. It’s a philosophy that applies to building AI systems in general, but is absolutely critical in the sciences.

Armed with that realization, I attended an AI for Science workshop at ICML, where I connected with a researcher from Caltech whose work aligned perfectly with this approach. After navigating the inevitable logistics, I began working with them, which leads me to the ongoing project I am incredibly proud of today: computational enzyme design and directed evolution.

Looking back, the irony is not lost on me. There was a point where I actively decided I would never do computer science (boring), chemistry (I wasn't very good at it), or biology (because I took math, and biology was "only for doctors"). Yet here I am, years later, applying AI to computational biochemistry.

The Problem Solver Identity?

Looking back at my trajectory, from autonomous pesticide-spraying robot to underwater navigation, thermal imaging, UWB sensors, phenotype-driven drug discovery, and now enzyme design the domains couldn't be more different. In every single one of them, there were parts I disliked and moments that made me wonder what I had signed up for. What saved me was learning not to associate myself with the type of problem I was solving, but rather the act of solving it. Shonda Rhimes (The brilliant mind behind Grey's Anatomy) does a lot more than just write, but her business card simply says 'storyteller.' because at her core, that is who she is. If I had a business card, it would say 'problem solver.' Yes, it’s a cliché. But just as there are many stories to tell, there are many problems to solve.
